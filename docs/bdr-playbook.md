**Signal Advisory — Business Development Representative**

# 01  The Mission
You are the front line of Signal Advisory. Every conversation you have is the first chance for a prospect to decide whether we are worth their time. You are not a script reader. You are a human being representing two other human beings, Brandon and Alanna, who built this business to give real companies real advice.
Your job is not to sell. Your job is to open the door, qualify whether there is a fit, and hand off warm meetings to Brandon or Alanna. That is it. You are not closing deals. You are not negotiating pricing. You are starting relationships.
The companies we want to reach are mid market and enterprise organizations in financial services, healthcare, manufacturing, distribution, retail, hospitality, faith, nonprofit, and AI infrastructure. They have between one hundred and five thousand employees. They are paying too much for connectivity and they do not know who to trust to help them fix it.
Your north star is fifty qualified meetings per month. That is the number that builds pipeline. That is the number that pays the bills. That is the number we measure you against.
# 02  The Voice
Signal Advisory sounds like a person. Not a brand. Not a corporation. Not a script.
### What Signal Advisory sounds like
- Conversational. Like you are talking to a neighbor over the fence, not presenting in a conference room.
- Direct. We tell the truth quickly. We do not pad our calls with niceties.
- Confident without being pushy. We know we have something valuable. We also know when to back off.
- Human. We laugh at ourselves. We acknowledge the awkwardness of cold calling. We treat people like people.
- Curious. We ask more than we tell. We want to understand the prospect is world before we offer a solution.
### What Signal Advisory does not sound like
- Corporate jargon. No synergies, no solutions, no optimized outcomes.
- Aggressive or high pressure. We are not car salespeople. We are not timeshare pitchers.
- Robotic. We do not read from a script word for word. We internalize the structure and let the conversation flow.
- Slick. We are not trying to wow anyone. We are trying to earn their trust.
### The golden rule
If what you are about to say would make you cringe if your mom heard it, do not say it.
# 03  The Daily Rhythm
Consistency beats intensity. A BDR who dials four hours every single day will crush one who sprints for ten hours on Monday and burns out by Wednesday.
## Your daily targets
- Sixty dials per day
- Fifteen live conversations per day
- Two qualified meetings booked per day
- Ten follow up emails sent per day
- Five LinkedIn connection requests per day (with a note)
## Your weekly targets
- Three hundred dials per week
- Seventy five conversations per week
- Ten qualified meetings booked per week
- One Loom video summary to Brandon every Friday
## Your daily schedule (Kansas City time zone)
Adjust if you are in a different time zone. The goal is to dial when the buyer is most likely to answer.
- 8:00 to 8:30 AM. Pipeline review, daily plan, warm up.
- 8:30 to 10:30 AM. Power dial block one. Two hours. No breaks.
- 10:30 to 11:00 AM. Email follow ups, CRM hygiene, notes.
- 11:00 AM to 1:00 PM. Power dial block two. Two hours. This is the golden window for decision makers.
- 1:00 to 2:00 PM. Lunch, walk, reset.
- 2:00 to 4:00 PM. Power dial block three. Last two hours. Catch people coming back from lunch.
- 4:00 to 5:00 PM. Admin, LinkedIn, research for tomorrow.
# 04  Pre Call Research
Three minutes of research before a call triples your chances of a meaningful conversation. Five minutes is better. Ten is usually wasted unless the account is huge.
## The three minute checklist
- Company website. What do they do? How many locations? Revenue ballpark?
- LinkedIn. Who is the person? How long have they been in the role?
- Recent news. Any press releases, funding, acquisitions, leadership changes in the last six months?
## The tell me something real test
Before you dial, make sure you can say something specific and true about the company in the first thirty seconds. Not a compliment. A fact. "I saw you opened a new location in Overland Park last month." "Your CEO just did a podcast on the supply chain stuff." This separates you from every other BDR calling that day.
# 05  Qualification Framework
We use a simplified version of BANT plus a gut check. If you can answer these five questions by the end of a first call, we have a qualified meeting.
## The five questions
- Budget. Do they have or could they have money to spend on connectivity decisions this year?
- Authority. Are they a decision maker, influencer, or gatekeeper? All three have value but we treat them differently.
- Need. Is there a real pain point? Something breaking? Something expensive? A contract coming due?
- Timeline. When would they actually make a change? Zero to six months is ideal. Six to eighteen months is worth nurturing. Longer than that, we set a calendar reminder and move on.
- Fit. Gut check. Do we actually have a solution? If the answer is no, pass them on and save everyone is time.
## What a qualified meeting looks like
- Calendar confirmed with date and time
- Right person or right influencer invited
- At least two of the five BANT questions confirmed
- A specific topic or problem agreed on for the meeting agenda
- Logged in CRM with tags and notes

# 06  Scripts by Persona
Nine personas. Nine different sets of priorities, pressures, and personalities. Learn the shape of each conversation. Do not memorize the words. Internalize the flow, and let your own voice carry it.
## Front Desk and Gatekeeper
The door before the door. Respect them or the door closes.
### Who they are
Receptionist, front desk attendant, main line answerer, executive assistant filtering incoming calls. Their job is to route legitimate business and filter sales noise. They are trained to say no. You win them by being human, respectful, and specific.
### What they actually care about
- Not getting yelled at by their boss for routing a bad sales call
- Being treated like a person, not an obstacle
- Quick, clear interactions so they can do the rest of their job
- Knowing who is actually calling and why, in plain English
### Opening (first ten seconds)
Hey, good morning. This is [Name] with Signal Advisory. Trying to catch [Person] real quick. Is she around this morning?
Note: Confidence, first name basis when possible, no nervous energy. You belong on this call. Act like it.
### The hook (if they give you thirty seconds)
Sure, happy to tell you. Brandon Murphy spent fifteen years inside Spectrum working with companies like yours. He is independent now and helping finance folks and IT folks compare their current carrier setup against the market. Quick fifteen minute conversation, totally free. What is the best way to get on her calendar?
### Qualifying questions
- Is she around this morning, or would later in the week be better?
- Would you rather I email you and you forward it along?
- Who handles your phone and internet vendor decisions these days?
- Is there someone in finance or IT who might be a better first call?
### The ask
What is the best way to get on her calendar? I can hold a fifteen minute slot next Tuesday or Thursday, whichever works.
### If they object
We are not interested.
Totally understand. Out of curiosity, who handles those vendor decisions now? I would want to make sure I am not barking up the wrong tree next time we talk. Appreciate it either way.
Send me an email and we will follow up.
Happy to do that. What is the best email? And can I get your name so I can make sure it lands in front of the right person? I will make it short. No forms, no links.
We already have someone.
Nice, makes sense. Most of the companies we help already have a provider, we are usually just a second opinion when something comes up for renewal. Mind if I ask who she works with currently? Just so I know the landscape.
She does not take unsolicited calls.
Makes sense, a lot of folks are that way these days. If I send a short intro email today, would you be willing to flag it for her? I will keep it under five sentences.
### What a good outcome looks like
You either get a calendar hold, a direct email address, or the name of the real decision maker inside the organization. All three are wins. A firm no with the right contact name is better than a maybe with no path forward.

## Office Manager and Admin
They know where the bodies are buried. Make them your friend.
### Who they are
Office Manager, Executive Assistant, Business Operations Coordinator, Administrative Director. Often the person who actually manages the phone vendor, watches the bills, or coordinates the IT vendor. Frequently a hidden decision maker in small and mid sized offices.
### What they actually care about
- Things running smoothly without constant fires
- Getting credit when they save the company money
- Being looped in on decisions they will have to support
- Not being talked down to or pitched hard
### Opening (first ten seconds)
Hey [Name], this is [BDR] with Signal Advisory, how is your morning going? I am hoping you can help me figure out who at [Company] handles your phone and internet vendors these days.
Note: Short, human, ask for help. Office managers respond to being treated as competent and important, because they are.
### The hook (if they give you thirty seconds)
Good deal. Reason I am calling, Brandon Murphy runs Signal Advisory. He spent fifteen years inside the carriers, Spectrum most recently, and went independent this spring. He helps companies like yours look at what they are paying for phones, internet, and security, and find real money. Usually ends up saving twenty to thirty percent. Thought it was worth a quick introduction.
### Qualifying questions
- Who is your main contact at your current provider?
- When was the last time anybody looked at what you are paying for phone or internet?
- Are there any contracts you know of that are coming up for renewal this year?
- Is there anything currently not working that we should know about? Dropped calls, slow internet, billing headaches?
### The ask
Would it make sense for me to put you on a quick intro call with Brandon next week? Fifteen minutes. If you want to loop in IT or finance, great. If not, you can just share what you learn after.
### If they object
I cannot make that call.
Totally get it. Who do you typically loop in for a decision like that? I can reach out to them directly and copy you so you are not out of the loop.
We just signed a contract.
Got it. That is actually a pretty common time to talk to us, because we can look at the contract together and see what is negotiable before you are too far into it. Either way I am happy to circle back in a few months. When does your term run?
We are too small for this.
I hear that a lot and it is usually not true. Some of our best clients are companies your size. Smaller companies actually tend to be paying more per user than the big guys because nobody ever pushed back on pricing. Want me to send a quick one pager?
### What a good outcome looks like
You find your champion. Office managers who like you become internal advocates. Even if they cannot sign, they can tee up the meeting, share the bill, warm up the CFO. Leave every call with their direct email and an open door.

## Controller
They think in dollars. Show them dollars.
### Who they are
Controller, Director of Finance, VP of Finance, sometimes Accounting Manager. They review the telecom bills every month, they feel the creep, and they never have time to do anything about it. Pain is spending too much with nothing to show for it.
### What they actually care about
- Reducing operating expense without breaking anything
- Visibility into what the company is actually spending on utilities and vendors
- Clean audit trails and renegotiated contracts
- Getting credit for finding savings the CFO did not know existed
### Opening (first ten seconds)
Hey [Name], [BDR] with Signal Advisory. Quick question. When is the last time anybody actually audited what [Company] is paying for phone and internet service?
Note: Controllers respect directness and respect numbers. Do not open with a pitch. Open with a diagnostic question.
### The hook (if they give you thirty seconds)
Here is why I am asking. Most controllers we talk to tell us their phone and internet bills have gone up fifteen to twenty percent over the last two years with no one in the office really watching it. We audit those contracts at no cost. We usually find twenty percent savings, sometimes more, and we recover billing errors and unused services. If we do not find savings, you pay nothing and we go away. Worth fifteen minutes?
### Qualifying questions
- Roughly what is your monthly spend on voice, data, and mobility combined?
- Are you still on MPLS, or have you moved to SD-WAN?
- Do you have visibility into what each site is paying individually?
- Any renegotiations coming up in the next six months?
### The ask
Let me put a quick meeting on the calendar with our founder Brandon Murphy. He ran enterprise accounts at Spectrum, so he will walk you through exactly what to look for. Fifteen minutes. No prep required. You bring a recent bill, he brings the market rates.
### If they object
We are fine, our spend is under control.
Good for you, that is rare. Out of curiosity, when is the last time you benchmarked your rates against the market? We can do that in about an hour, free, and either confirm you are getting a fair deal or show you where you are being overpaid. Either way you win.
We just went through renewal.
Makes sense. Two questions then. First, did you shop it or did you renew with the same vendor? Second, do you have a copy of the contract? Sometimes we find clauses that cost companies serious money over the term that are totally negotiable if you catch them early.
Not interested.
Fair. Last thing, I promise. If I send you a one pager showing the average savings we found for mid market companies last quarter, would you take five minutes to look at it? If it is boring you delete it. If not, we talk.
### What a good outcome looks like
Controllers convert at a higher rate than almost any title because the value prop is provable in dollars. Leave every call with a scheduled meeting, a bill shared, or a firm callback date.

## Owner
Time poor. Wants simplicity. Respects directness.
### Who they are
Small business owner, founder, family business owner, franchise operator. Usually wearing many hats. Does not want to become an expert in telecom. Wants someone trustworthy to just handle it.
### What they actually care about
- Uptime and the business running
- Employees able to do their jobs without complaining about the phones
- Not getting ripped off by vendors
- Simplicity and fewer people to manage
### Opening (first ten seconds)
Hey [Name], [BDR] with Signal Advisory. I will be short because I know you are busy. Are you the person who deals with the phone and internet bill for [Company], or is that someone else on your team?
Note: Owners appreciate respect for their time. Never waste their opening words on niceties. Get to the point.
### The hook (if they give you thirty seconds)
Good. Real quick. My team, we take the whole phone, internet, mobile, and security picture off your plate. One advisor, every carrier shopped. If something breaks, you call us, not the carrier support line. Most owners we work with save money and stop worrying about this stuff. Thought it was worth fifteen minutes.
### Qualifying questions
- How many employees and locations are we talking about?
- What is not working right now? Anything driving you nuts?
- Who is your current provider for phones and for internet?
- If someone could just handle all this for you, would that be worth exploring?
### The ask
I can hold a fifteen minute slot with our founder Brandon Murphy this week. He worked enterprise accounts at Spectrum for years. He will take a look at what you have, tell you if there is anything we would change, and if not, he will leave you alone. No pressure.
### If they object
I do not have time.
I get that. Fifteen minutes could save you five hours of fighting with your current vendor over the next year. Can I send you a one pager and you pick a time later this month?
We use [Vendor] and they are fine.
Glad to hear it. Most of the time when we come in, people are fine, they are just overpaying. Would you be open to a free audit of your current bill? Literally no obligation. If you are getting a great deal, we tell you and go away. If not, we show you where.
Not right now.
Understood. What would change in the next three to six months that would make this worth a look? Renewal coming up? Adding a location? Let me note it and circle back at the right time.
### What a good outcome looks like
Owners either say yes fast or no fast. Good outcome is either a calendar hold or a clear callback trigger. Do not leave without one of the two.

## CEO
Strategic outcomes. Not features. Not products.
### Who they are
Chief Executive Officer, President, Managing Partner. Thinking about growth, margin, risk, and whether their team can execute. Does not care about SD-WAN versus MPLS. Cares about whether connectivity is helping or hurting the business.
### What they actually care about
- Growth, margin, and competitive positioning
- Whether their infrastructure is a liability or an advantage
- Uptime tied to revenue
- How to scale without adding headcount for vendor management
### Opening (first ten seconds)
Hey [Name], [BDR] with Signal Advisory. Brandon Murphy asked me to get on your radar. He spent fifteen years inside Spectrum and the other big carriers, went independent this spring. Worth a quick introduction?
Note: CEOs respond to peer positioning. Brandon as a fellow operator, not a salesperson. Borrow the credibility carefully.
### The hook (if they give you thirty seconds)
The reason Brandon wanted me to reach out. Most companies your size are paying twenty to thirty percent more than they should on connectivity because nobody is shopping the market on their behalf. The carriers know their enterprise sales rep turns over every eighteen months. That is why rates keep creeping up. Brandon sits on your side of the table, shops every carrier, and stays on your account for the life of the relationship. Fifteen minute intro?
### Qualifying questions
- How is your organization thinking about network and connectivity over the next couple years?
- Any plans to open new locations, acquire, or consolidate?
- Who on your team owns the carrier relationships today?
- When is the last time you felt like you got a straight answer from your carrier?
### The ask
Let me put fifteen minutes on the calendar with Brandon this week or next. No decks. No sales pitch. Just a conversation about what is working and what is not.
### If they object
I have a team for that.
Of course. Brandon is usually a resource to those teams, not a replacement for them. Your IT folks and finance folks often tell us we make their job easier because we shop the market they do not have time to. Worth the intro so he is in your Rolodex if you ever need a second opinion?
We are happy with our current vendor.
Makes sense. The best time to build a relationship is when you do not need one. Brandon stays on accounts long term. A fifteen minute intro now means when something breaks in eighteen months, you have already got someone to call.
Send me something in writing.
Absolutely. Prefer email or InMail on LinkedIn? I will keep it brief. Should I expect a response, or is there an assistant I should copy?
### What a good outcome looks like
CEOs either refer you down to the right subordinate or accept the intro. Both are wins. If they refer you down, ask for a warm handoff rather than a cold name.

## CTO
Technical authority. Wants signal, not noise.
### Who they are
Chief Technology Officer. Owns the architecture. Cares about scale, reliability, security posture, and whether the platform is ready for what comes next. Does not want to be educated, wants to be challenged with good thinking.
### What they actually care about
- Architectural decisions that compound well
- Security, resilience, performance
- Not getting locked into vendors that cannot scale
- Whether the vendor rep actually knows what they are talking about
### Opening (first ten seconds)
Hey [Name], [BDR] with Signal Advisory. Got a minute? I am not calling to pitch you. I am calling to see if our founder and you should meet.
Note: CTOs smell sales pressure from a mile away. Disarm it immediately. Then earn thirty seconds with substance.
### The hook (if they give you thirty seconds)
Brandon Murphy ran enterprise accounts at Spectrum for years. He is independent now. The reason this is worth a conversation. Every mid market CTO we talk to is thinking about three things right now. SASE consolidation, AI infrastructure connectivity, and whether they are overpaying on legacy MPLS. Brandon has deep patterns on all three, across every major carrier. Thought it might be worth a peer level conversation, not a sales meeting.
### Qualifying questions
- Where are you on the SASE or zero trust journey?
- Still running MPLS anywhere, or fully migrated to SD-WAN?
- Any AI workloads putting pressure on your WAN or data center connectivity?
- Who is your current primary carrier, and how is that relationship going?
### The ask
Would thirty minutes with Brandon be useful? He does not pitch. He brings patterns from across the market and lets you pull what is useful. If it is not valuable, you have lost thirty minutes. If it is, you have got a new advisor.
### If they object
We have a great architect on staff.
Good, that is how it should be. Brandon is not an architect replacement. He is a market benchmark. Your architect probably does not have time to shop every carrier every quarter. That is what Brandon does, and he gives your architect the data to make better decisions.
Not a priority right now.
Fair. What is on the priority list this year? If any of them touch connectivity, security, or voice, worth flagging Brandon so he is there when you need him. No pressure to meet today.
I am not taking new vendor meetings.
Completely understand. We do not consider this a vendor meeting. It is a peer conversation. If at the end you want us to go away, we do. Would a ten minute conversation on LinkedIn DMs work better than a full meeting?
### What a good outcome looks like
CTOs respect substance and hate fluff. Good outcome is thirty minutes with Brandon, or an introduction to a VP or Director on the team. Never leave with nothing.

Operational leader. Answering to the CIO and the business.

## IT Manager
Hands on. Fixing things. Wants tools, not talks.
### Who they are
IT Manager, Network Manager, Systems Administrator, sometimes the solo IT person at a small company. Responsible for tickets, fixes, and keeping the business running. Limited budget authority but massive influence over what the team buys next.
### What they actually care about

### What a good outcome looks like
IT managers are the sleeper champions. They rarely sign, but they always tell their boss to meet. Get them a fifteen minute call, arm them with a one pager, and let them advocate up the chain.


# 07  Voicemail Scripts
Ninety percent of your calls will go to voicemail. A good voicemail gets a call back or an email reply. A bad voicemail trains them to ignore you forever. Keep it under thirty seconds. Always leave your name, company, phone, and a specific reason.
## Voicemail script 01: The benchmark hook (Controller, CFO, VP Finance)
Hey [Name], this is [BDR] with Signal Advisory. I was calling because most controllers at companies your size are paying fifteen to twenty percent more for phones and internet than they need to, and nobody is really watching it. We do a free audit that finds the money in about an hour. If that sounds worth a conversation, give me a call back at [Number], or I will follow up with an email. Thanks.
## Voicemail script 02: The advisor hook (CEO, CIO, Owner)
Hey [Name], [BDR] with Signal Advisory. Brandon Murphy asked me to get on your radar. He spent fifteen years inside Spectrum working with companies like yours, now he is independent. Wanted to see if a fifteen minute intro would be worth your time. If yes, call me back at [Number]. If not, I will swing by your inbox with a short note. Thanks.
## Voicemail script 03: The operational hook (IT Director, IT Manager, CTO)
Hey [Name], [BDR] with Signal Advisory. Cold call so I will be quick. We take the carrier management headache off IT teams like yours. One advisor, every carrier, we fight the vendors so you do not have to. Worth fifteen minutes. Call me at [Number] or I will send a short email. Have a good one.

# 09  Objection Handling Library
Objections are not rejections. They are requests for more information. Listen for what is underneath the words. Most of the time the real objection is not what they said first. Here are the ten most common objections you will hear and how Signal Advisory handles them.
### 01  "We are not interested."
Happy to step back. Out of curiosity, who does handle carrier decisions now? Want to make sure I am not reaching out to the wrong person next time something changes on your end.
### 02  "We just renewed."
Actually a great time to talk. Most companies do not look at the contract carefully enough. Could we just do a five minute review of the terms? Sometimes there are clauses worth negotiating even mid term.
### 03  "Send me an email."
Absolutely. What is the best address? And if I could ask, what would make the email worth your reply? I would rather send you something valuable than something that lands in spam.
### 04  "We have a great current provider."
Glad to hear it. We are not asking you to replace them. Think of us as a second opinion when big decisions come up. Carriers count on companies not having anyone in their corner. Fifteen minutes to have someone in your corner, no strings.
### 05  "Too busy right now."
I completely understand. What is a better time to circle back? Two weeks? A month? I will put it on my calendar and check in then.
### 06  "We handle this internally."
Most companies your size do. The question is whether your internal person has time to shop every carrier every quarter. We are a force multiplier for internal teams, not a replacement.
### 07  "What is this costing me?"
Nothing. We are paid by the carriers on the backend when you choose to move forward with a service. You never pay us. Our advice is free. Our audit is free. Our negotiation work is free. The only thing this costs you is fifteen minutes.
### 08  "How did you get my name?"
Public data, LinkedIn, industry research. You came up because of your role at [Company]. If you are not the right person, just tell me who is and I will stop calling you.
### 09  "Are you a robot?"
No. [Short human comment about the weather, the day, or something real]. How is your week going?
### 10  "I just do not trust phone calls from numbers I do not recognize."
Completely fair, me neither most days. Want me to send a short LinkedIn message instead so you can verify me? Happy to work on whatever channel feels right.
