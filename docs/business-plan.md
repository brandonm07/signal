## 01 — The Business Model

Three revenue streams operating together. Not one business with side income. Three reinforcing layers that compound off the same client relationships.

### Layer one / Sub-agent residuals

The compounding engine. Every deal sold through master agent partners (Avant, Telarus, Sandler Partners) pays a monthly residual for the life of the client contract, typically three to five years. This is the moat. This is the retirement plan. You build it once and it pays forever as long as the client keeps the service.

- **Economics.** Residuals typically run four to ten percent of client monthly recurring charge. A $25,000 monthly client pays roughly $1,500 per month in residuals, indefinitely.
- **Target.** Build a residual book paying $20,000+ per month by end of year one.

### Layer two / Advisory services

Billable expertise for clients who want strategic guidance without a specific product purchase attached. Vendor evaluation, contract negotiation, architecture planning, telecom expense audits, carrier strategy reviews.

Start with hourly billing. Graduate mature clients to monthly retainers. Retainer clients pay a fixed monthly fee for a defined scope of advisory work regardless of hours consumed.

- **Economics.** Hourly rates $200 to $400 depending on scope. Monthly retainers $2,000 to $10,000 depending on client size and scope.
- **Target.** Three paying advisory clients by end of year one. One on retainer.

### Layer three / Managed services capacity

Operational execution on behalf of clients. Implementation project management, vendor escalation handling, ongoing account management, invoice auditing, carrier portal administration. Delivered under monthly service agreements.

- **Economics.** Monthly fees $1,500 to $5,000 per client depending on scope and footprint.
- **Target.** Two managed services clients signed by end of year one.

### Why the triple stack matters

Residuals pay slowly but compound forever. Advisory pays fast but requires time for time. Managed services builds recurring cash flow independent of new deal sales. Together they smooth the cash curve, deepen client relationships, and create multiple reasons for a client to stay. Most brokers only do layer one. You beat them by stacking all three.

## 02 — The Twenty Steps

Ground up. No assumptions. The order matters. Do them in sequence. Do not skip ahead until the prior step is actually done.

### Step 01 — Define the revenue mix commitment

You have chosen the triple stack. Sub-agent residuals. Advisory services. Managed services. The commitment here is how you split your time across the three. Recommended year one allocation: sixty percent residual sales activity, thirty percent advisory delivery, ten percent managed services setup. Write this down. Revisit quarterly.

### Step 02 — Build the financial model before the brand

Twenty four month projection. Three revenue streams modeled separately. Monthly expenses. Break even month. Cash runway required. How much money needs to come out each month for the household. What is the funding gap in month one through month six, and how is it covered. No logo matters if the math does not work.

### Step 03 — Lock the capital plan

Three numbers. How much cash lives in the business account on day one. How much personal runway funds the household through a slow quarter. Where that money comes from. Savings, HELOC, spouse income, family loan, SBA. Get this locked before anyone resigns from anything.

### Step 04 — Choose the legal structure

LLC is the right answer. Two member LLC taxed as a partnership until you clear about $80,000 in profit, then S corp election to reduce self employment tax. C corp only if you are raising outside money or planning a strategic exit. One hour with a small business CPA is all you need to finalize this. Budget: $200 to $400.

### Step 05 — File the entity in Missouri

Articles of organization filed through the Missouri Secretary of State online portal. Fee: $105. You can act as your own registered agent if you are fine with a public address, or pay Northwest Registered Agent about $125 per year for privacy.

### Step 06 — Get the EIN

Free from the IRS at irs.gov. Fifteen minutes online. Required to open a business bank account, hire anyone, and get paid by any master agent. Zero cost.

### Step 07 — Open a business bank account

Chase Business Complete, Bank of America Business Advantage, or a local Kansas City credit union. Free checking accounts are widely available. Bring articles of organization, EIN letter, and drivers license. Never commingle personal and business money. Not once. Ever. Commingling erases the liability protection you just filed for.

### Step 08 — Stand up accounting (free tier)

Wave is fully free for accounting, invoicing, and receipt tracking. Zero monthly cost. Good through the first $100,000 of revenue. Connect the business bank account on day one. Categorize every transaction. When you outgrow Wave, graduate to QuickBooks Online at $30 per month. Find a CPA who works with small service businesses before you have a mess to clean up. Budget: $1,500 for the first year of light CPA work.

### Step 09 — Insurance (deferred decision)

You have made the call to defer errors and omissions and general liability insurance until the business is delivering proprietary technology or handling client data directly. This is a conscious tradeoff. One consideration before finalizing: most master agents require E&O coverage as a condition of their sub-agent agreements, typically $1 million in coverage. If a specific master agent requires it at signing, budget $800 to $1,500 per year at that moment. Revisit at the master agent contract phase, not earlier.

### Step 10 — Understand the master agent landscape

The seven serious master agents are Avant Communications, Telarus, Sandler Partners, Bridgepointe Technologies, PlanetOne, Intelisys (ScanSource), and Technology Services Distribution. Each has different carrier rosters, commission structures, agent portals, and cultural fits. Interview at least three before signing any. Ask specifically about commission transparency, SPIF program details, dispute resolution, training, and marketing support.

### Step 11 — Apply to your first master agent

Start with one primary agent. Add a second within 90 days. Add a third by month six. Applications take days to weeks. You will need entity formation docs, a basic website (even a holding page), and either industry experience or a sponsor.

### Step 12 — Define the product portfolio

Even when a master agent carries 300 providers, you sell fewer than ten core offerings in year one. Focus on what you can sell confidently. Recommended starter portfolio: SD-WAN, UCaaS, dedicated internet, cybersecurity (SASE), and mobility. Five products. Deep expertise. Expand in year two.

### Step 13 — Write the target market definition

> Mid market enterprises in manufacturing, distribution, financial services, and healthcare, with 100 to 2,000 employees, multi-site operations, based in the central United States.

Write it tighter than that if you can. Revisit quarterly.

### Step 14 — Get trained (free)

Every master agent provides free training to onboarded agents. Every carrier offers free partner certifications (AT&T Partner Exchange, Lumen Channel Partner, Verizon Partner Program, Comcast Business). Earn them. Zero cost. High credibility return.

### Step 15 — Build the operational backbone

See the next page for the free tier stack and the minimum viable paid stack. Total monthly tool cost either $0 or $42 depending on tier. Budget no more than $500 on tools for the entire first year. You do not need Sales Navigator, PandaDoc, ZoomInfo, or Apollo until revenue justifies it.

### Step 16 — Get the legal templates (low cost)

Three documents needed before the first client conversation. A Master Service Agreement or Letter of Engagement. An NDA template. A Referral Partner Agreement. Free to lowest cost sources: LawDepot, LegalTemplates, and SignWell have free or low cost template libraries. Master agent communities often share MSA templates at no charge. One time attorney review of your final templates: $500 to $1,000 on Upwork.

### Step 17 — Set up tax and compliance

Register with Missouri Department of Revenue for sales tax if you sell taxable services. Set up quarterly estimated tax payments with your CPA so you do not get hit in April. Put 25 to 30 percent of every commission check into a separate savings account for taxes immediately. Track all business expenses through Wave. If you have no employees and no contractors, you are in the simplest possible tax posture.

### Step 18 — Build a basic brand identity (DIY)

Name, logo, color system, typography, tagline, business cards, one page website, email signature, LinkedIn company page. All doable on free tools. Canva Free for logo, cards, one pager. Framer Free for website. LinkedIn Company Page free. Gmail signature builder free. If you want a designer, budget cap $500 on Contra or Fiverr Pro. You do not need to spend thousands on branding before revenue.

### Step 19 — Write your first year operating plan

One page. Revenue target. Expense budget. Three to five key initiatives. Monthly review cadence. Quarterly goal reset. See the next section for the complete template.

### Step 20 — Answer the why question

Customers, carriers, and partners will ask some version of: Why you specifically. Why this moment. Why should I trust you with my business. If you cannot answer each in two sentences, you are not ready to open. Write the answers. Memorize them. Test them on three people who will tell you the truth. Refine until they land. This is the difference between a hobby and a business.

## 05 — Where Alanna is Right to Flag Gaps

A candid acknowledgment. The earlier sales playbook focused heavily on sales execution and skipped or skimmed foundational pieces a business plan should cover. This document closes those gaps.

- **Financial modeling.** The earlier plan projected revenue without modeling expenses, cash flow timing, or tax impact. This document makes the expense budget explicit and builds in the quarterly tax reserve.
- **Capital planning.** The earlier plan assumed self funding implicitly. This document calls it out as a decision that must be made explicitly before anything else moves forward.
- **Legal templates.** The earlier plan skipped contracts entirely. This document names the three templates required (MSA, NDA, Referral Agreement) and sources them at low cost.
- **Tax strategy.** The earlier plan did not cover S corp election timing, quarterly estimates, or tax reserves. This document does.
- **Operating rhythm.** The earlier plan mentioned a weekly rhythm but did not specify a monthly or quarterly review cadence. This document installs both.
- **Business model clarity.** The earlier plan described sub-agent work only. This document reflects the full triple stack: residuals, advisory, and managed services.
